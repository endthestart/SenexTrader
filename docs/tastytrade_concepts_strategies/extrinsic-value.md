# Extrinsic Value

Source: https://www.tastylive.com/concepts-strategies/extrinsic-value

Extrinsic value, also known as "time value," is the portion of an option's premium that exceeds its intrinsic value. It reflects the possibility that an option could become in-the-money (ITM) before expiration.

**How Extrinsic Value Works:**
Extrinsic value is influenced by several factors:
*   **Time to Expiration:** Options with more time until expiration have greater extrinsic value because there's a higher chance they could become ITM.
*   **Implied Volatility:** Higher implied volatility in the underlying asset increases extrinsic value, as it suggests a greater probability of the option moving ITM.
*   **Interest Rates and Dividends:** Higher interest rates generally increase the extrinsic value of call options and decrease it for put options. Expected dividends typically decrease call option extrinsic value and increase put option extrinsic value.

**Calculating Extrinsic Value:**
To calculate extrinsic value, subtract the option's intrinsic value from its total market price (premium).
*   **Intrinsic Value:** For call options, this is the amount by which the underlying asset's price exceeds the strike price. For put options, it's the amount by which the strike price exceeds the underlying asset's price. If an option is out-of-the-money (OTM), its intrinsic value is zero.
*   **Extrinsic Value:** Market Price - Intrinsic Value. If an option is OTM, its entire value is extrinsic.

**Example:**
For a call option with a strike price of $50, an underlying stock price of $55, and a market value of $7:
1.  **Intrinsic Value:** $55 (stock price) - $50 (strike price) = $5.00.
2.  **Extrinsic Value:** $7 (market price) - $5 (intrinsic value) = $2.00.

**Which Options Have the Most Extrinsic Value?**
At-the-money (ATM) options (where the strike price equals the underlying asset's current price) typically have the most extrinsic value due to the highest uncertainty about their expiration outcome. Options with longer times to expiration and higher implied volatility also have greater extrinsic value.

Both OTM and ITM options can have extrinsic value. OTM options consist entirely of extrinsic value, while ITM options retain some extrinsic value because of the remaining uncertainty about whether they will stay ITM until expiration.
